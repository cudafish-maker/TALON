Segment 02 — The Enemy Within (The Platform)

They're Not On Your Side.
They Never Were.

Let's talk about the other threat. Not the weather. Not the disaster. The platform.

Right now, every message you send, every location you share, every status update you post — it lives on someone else's server. A corporation whose name you recognize but whose interests are not yours. A jurisdiction you did not choose. A terms-of-service agreement you accepted by clicking a button at 1 AM.

That corporation will sell your data. It will hand it over when asked. It will shut down, pivot, get acquired, change its terms, or simply decide that you are not worth keeping. And when the disaster comes and you need your coordination tools the most, those tools will be exactly as available as that corporation decides they should be.

The TAK Server Problem

Segment 03 — How TALON Works

One Node. One Community.
One Network You Own.

TALON runs on Reticulum — a mesh networking protocol built specifically for the conditions that break everything else. Low bandwidth. No central infrastructure. Intermittent links. Hostile environments. It routes to cryptographic identities, not to IP addresses. Your address on the network is a hash of who you are, not where you happen to be standing. It never changes. The network finds you.

When you run TALON, you are not a passive subscriber on someone else's system. You are a node in the mesh. Your machine can relay traffic for other nodes nearby. The network gets stronger, more resilient, and more capable of reaching further with every device that joins it. You are not consuming infrastructure. You are building it.

The Operational Picture

TALON gives your community a shared operational picture — live, structured, encrypted. Situation reports carry a type, a location, a time, and an operator attribution. Urgent ones surface on the dashboard immediately. They can reference assets, link to missions, carry follow-up chains. Not a group chat. A structured record that can be acted on.

The map carries live overlays: assets, assignments, zones, routes, waypoints, SITREP locations. Rendered on real base layers — street, topographic, satellite. When you drop a marker for a vehicle, it looks like a vehicle. Unverified assets stay unverified until a second party confirms them. The distinction between a claim and a verified fact matters when you are sending people based on it.

Missions give purpose to work. Assignments are the work. The assignment board shows who is committed to what, where the gaps are, and whether anything is actually moving. Operators carry declared skills — medical, logistics, search, rescue, communications — because in a crisis, knowing who has what capability is the operational picture.

Segment 04 — Asset Security

What Happens When a Device
Falls Into Enemy Hands.

The Federal Network Authority wants you to be informed about this, because good citizens plan for failure.

If a TALON device is seized, the adversary acquires an encrypted SQLCipher database. The key to that database is derived from your passphrase using Argon2id — a memory-hard function specifically designed to make brute-force attacks take an unacceptable amount of time and resources. The identity files on disk are separately encrypted. Without the passphrase, the adversary holds an expensive brick.

Meanwhile, back at command, the server operator has already initiated hard revocation. This is not a flag flip. It is a three-step irreversible destruction sequence:

Step one: Database shred. The operator's record is marked revoked and their network identity hash is cleared from the server database entirely — not archived, not suspended. Gone. Outstanding enrollment tokens are simultaneously invalidated.

Step two: Identity burn. The cached identity file is overwritten and deleted from disk. Even if the device comes back online, the server will not recognize it.

Step three: Group key rotation. Every remaining active operator receives a new group encryption key on their next sync. Whatever the adversary retained from the compromised device cannot decrypt a single byte of traffic generated after rotation.

An operator who suspects their own device is compromised may initiate self-revocation from the client. This stops all sync, closes active network links, destroys the local identity files, clears enrollment records, and marks the outbound queue revoked. The device goes dark. Fresh enrollment from clean hardware is required to return to the network.

"A centralized server that holds your entire operational picture is a single target. TALON gives them nothing but an encrypted brick and a network that has already moved on without them."

Segment 05 — Enrollment and Civic Duty

Joining the Network Is
Not a Sign-Up Form.

There is no button that says "Create Account." There is no email confirmation. There is no platform that owns your identity and lends it back to you in exchange for agreeing to their terms.

To join a TALON network, a server operator — a neighbor, a colleague, a community member who has accepted responsibility for maintaining the infrastructure — generates a one-time enrollment token and hands it to you through a channel outside the software. You enter it once. It is consumed. It cannot be reused. It cannot be guessed. It is the digital equivalent of a handshake between people who have looked each other in the eye.

This is by design. A network that allows anonymous self-enrollment can be flooded and corrupted by anyone. A network that ties identity to a phone number can be disrupted by whoever controls the phone company. TALON ties identity to a cryptographic keypair generated on your device, and to the explicit decision of a human being who vouched for you.

Operators hold a lease — a time-bounded authorization renewed by the server authority. When it expires, the client soft-locks. You are told. There is no mystery. There is no shadow-banning. The system does not pretend you have access you no longer hold. The audit log stands. The record is clear.

Visible authority is accountable authority. We at the Federal Network Authority believe this strongly and suggest you consider it as well.
