The entropy of the world is increasing. Our ability to communicate freely is degrading.

More and more, every detail of our private lives is owned by corporations and governments that have shown — time and time again — they do not have our best interests in mind. The tools we rely on to stay connected are not ours. The data we generate does not belong to us. The infrastructure that carries our voices runs on someone else's hardware, under someone else's terms, subject to someone else's decisions about when and whether it keeps working.

Public infrastructure is eroding. In rural areas, the time it takes to receive aid after a disaster can simply be too long. Not because people don't care. Because the systems we rely on were not built for that moment. They were built for the other moments — the quiet ones, when nothing is wrong, when the towers are up and the servers are running and somebody somewhere is paying the bill.

So let me ask you something.

What if you could contact your neighbor, even when the cell towers are down — even when the internet itself is down?

What if you owned the data that you send and receive?

What if that data was encrypted — actually encrypted, not “we use HTTPS” encrypted?

What if you could see live status from responders in a casualty scenario, from a field with no cellular coverage?

What if it was free?

That is what TALON is built to be.

The Tools We Already Have, and What They Assume

There are existing tools for this kind of work. ATAK — the Android Team Awareness Kit — is the most widely known. ATOC provides centralized command-and-control infrastructure. Both are serious software used by serious people doing serious work. This is not an argument that those tools are bad. It is an argument that they were built on assumptions that do not hold when things go wrong at scale.

ATAK started as a military program inside the Department of Defense. It was built for warfighters who have satellites, MANET radios, dedicated IT support, and an unbroken chain of logistics behind them. Civilian derivatives — CivTAK, iTAK, WinTAK — have found real use in search and rescue, fire response, and emergency management. Communities are doing important work with them right now.

But look at what those tools assume when you deploy them.

They assume you have a TAK Server. You can self-host it — which is genuinely good — but you need IP connectivity to reach it. Your field devices need to talk to that server over a network. When you configure ATAK for a field deployment, your starting assumption is that the network exists and your job is to build capability on top of it. When the network is what has failed, you are starting from a broken foundation.

ATOC follows the same pattern. The operational picture lives in a central system. The feeds come in over IP. The coordination model assumes that the hub is reachable, that the links stay up, and that somebody maintains the server. These are reasonable assumptions for the scenarios these tools were designed for. They are not reasonable assumptions for the scenarios TALON is designed for.

The Data Question

Even in the best case — self-hosted TAK Server, competent admin, proper network — your operational data is centralized. The operator positions, the tracks, the SITREPs, the chat: all of it lives on that server. If the server is compromised, the data is compromised. If someone gets access to it, they have your entire operational picture. Who was where, doing what, talking to whom, when.

For a military unit, this is a managed risk. There are clearances, physical security, and a chain of custody for that hardware. For a community mutual aid network, a neighborhood emergency response team, or a rural search-and-rescue unit — you probably do not have those things. And if you are using a cloud-hosted TAK option, your operational picture is on hardware you have never seen, in a data center you do not control, accessible to people you have never met under terms you agreed to by clicking a button.

The tools we use to coordinate in a crisis should not create a new liability in the process of solving the old one.

There is also the question of origin. Software that was built for military use carries the assumptions of that origin into everything it does — the interface metaphors, the data model, the mental framing of who is a "target" and who is a "friendly." Civilian emergency coordination is not a military operation. The people you are coordinating with are your neighbors. The framing matters.

What It Actually Costs to Run

The software comparison above matters. The hardware comparison is where it gets concrete.

Running a TAK Server means running a server. The official TAK Server is a Java application that wants four CPU cores, eight gigabytes of RAM, and fifty gigabytes of storage to be comfortable. FreeTAKServer is lighter, but still expects two cores and four gigabytes of RAM at minimum. Either way, you need a machine that runs continuously — twenty-four hours a day, seven days a week — because a coordination server that is off when you need it is not a coordination server. For most volunteer organizations, that means a cloud virtual private server. Budget twenty to forty dollars a month, plus someone who knows how to configure SSL certificates, open ports, manage DNS, and keep the instance updated. That person has to exist, and has to stay involved.

If you want to take TAK Server into the field on local infrastructure — a laptop or a mini-PC — you need IP networking between your server and your field devices, which typically means a WiFi access point or a mobile hotspot. When the hotspot runs out of data or the access point loses power, the server is unreachable and your operators lose the shared picture.

A TALON server runs on a Raspberry Pi. A current Raspberry Pi 4 with four gigabytes of RAM costs roughly fifty-five dollars. It draws five to eight watts. It fits in a jacket pocket. It runs for hours on a USB battery pack and indefinitely on a small solar panel. Setup is installing Python dependencies and running the server process — no SSL configuration, no cloud account, no monthly bill.

The mesh nodes that carry traffic between operators are the same class of hardware. A Pi or any low-power Linux device paired with an RNode LoRa radio — which runs between fifty and eighty dollars depending on the band and form factor — gives you a relay point that extends the network's reach by several kilometers in terrain, and up to fifteen kilometers line of sight. Stack a few of these across a search area and you have mesh coverage with no cellular dependency and no internet dependency, powered by hardware that costs less than a decent pair of boots.

What TALON Does Instead

TALON communicates over Reticulum — a network stack designed specifically for the conditions everyone else treats as edge cases. Low bandwidth. High latency. Intermittent links. No central infrastructure. Reticulum routes to cryptographic identities, not IP addresses. It encrypts everything, always, as a foundational requirement rather than an add-on. It runs over LoRa radios, serial connections, Yggdrasil, I2P, packet radio, or plain TCP when that is available.

This means that when the cellular network goes down, LoRa radios can carry the operational picture across kilometers of difficult terrain on battery power. When the internet is filtered or congested, alternative paths take over automatically. When there is nothing available except a wire between two machines — that wire is enough. The software does not know or care what medium it is running over. It knows destinations. The network finds the path.

Your data never leaves your device in an unencrypted form. The local database is SQLCipher-encrypted and unlocked only with your passphrase. Your cryptographic identity is a keypair generated on your machine. The server holds your RNS hash as your enrollment record — it does not hold your private key, because your private key never leaves your machine. There is no central database of operational pictures for someone to breach. There is no cloud option that quietly routes your data through a jurisdiction you did not choose.

The Enrollment Ceremony

Joining a TALON network is not a self-service action. There is no signup form. The server operator — the person who has accepted responsibility for running the coordination infrastructure for your organization — generates a one-time enrollment token and hands it to you through a channel outside the software. You enter it once. It is consumed. It cannot be reused or guessed.

This is deliberate. A network that allows anonymous self-enrollment can be flooded, spoofed, and corrupted by anyone. A network that ties identity to a phone number or email can be disrupted by whoever controls those registries. TALON ties identity to a cryptographic keypair and to the explicit trust decision of a human being who vouched for you. That accountability goes both directions.

The Operational Picture

What TALON gives you, practically, is a live shared picture of what is happening — one that works when the infrastructure does not.

Situation reports are structured: a type, a location, a time, the operator who filed it, a narrative. Urgent ones surface on the dashboard immediately. They can carry follow-up chains, reference assets, link to active missions. This is not a group chat with a pin dropped in it. It is a structured record that can be reasoned about, prioritized, and acted on without someone having to read back through a thread to figure out what is still current.

The map carries live operational overlays — assets, operator assignments, zones, routes, waypoints, SITREP locations — rendered on top of real base layers: OpenStreetMap, topographic, satellite. When you place a marker for a vehicle, it looks like a vehicle. When you draw a patrol route, it looks like a route. Assets remain unverified until a second party or the server authority confirms them. The distinction between "someone said this vehicle is here" and "this vehicle has been verified at this location" is small and critical when you are sending people based on it.

Missions give purpose to tasks. Assignments are the tasks. The assignment board shows who is committed to what, where the gaps are, whether the work is actually moving. Operators declare their skills — medical, logistics, communications, search, rescue — not as a preference setting but as information that the people coordinating with them genuinely need.

Authority, Visibility, and the Surveillance Problem

Every coordination tool makes choices about authority — who can see what, who can assign whom, who can remove someone from the network. TALON makes those choices visible rather than hiding them in an algorithm.

The server operator has explicit authority: enrollment, lease renewal, revocation, asset verification, audit logs. This authority is not hidden from the people it governs. The audit log stands. When a lease is not renewed, the operator is told. When a revocation happens, it is not a mystery. There is no shadow-banning. The system does not maintain the fiction that you have trust you no longer hold.

The harder question is the community safety layer — the assignment boards, check-in workflows, location pings, welfare tracking. These features exist because communities under stress need to care for their members as much as they need to track their logistics. But community safety tools have a history of becoming surveillance tools the moment the incentive shifts. A location ping that shows where someone is can be welfare or it can be a leash. TALON's principle is consent and context: location pings are initiated by operators, not pushed by the server. Check-ins happen within defined mission contexts. The data does not leave your encrypted local database. Visibility is bounded by operational purpose and nothing more.

When a Device Is Seized

In any operational context where the stakes are real, you have to think about what happens when hardware ends up in the wrong hands. A device left at a scene. A vehicle checkpoint. A lost phone. The question is not if this can happen. It is what an adversary actually gets when it does.

On a seized TALON device, they get an encrypted SQLCipher database. The key to that database is derived from your passphrase using Argon2id — a deliberately slow, memory-hard function designed to make brute-force attacks expensive. The identity files on disk are also encrypted, wrapped in a PyNaCl SecretBox keyed from the unlocked database. Without the passphrase, the database is an opaque blob. The identity files are noise. There is no plaintext to read, no operational picture to reconstruct, no operator list to extract.

That covers the device itself. But a compromised asset is also an active identity on the network, and that needs to be dealt with separately.

Hard Revocation

When the server operator revokes a compromised identity, it is not a soft lock or a flag flip. It is a three-step destruction, in order, intentionally irreversible.

First, the database shred. The operator's record is marked revoked and their RNS identity hash is cleared from the database entirely — not archived, cleared — so that hash cannot be reused in a new enrollment. Any outstanding enrollment tokens tied to that operator are invalidated at the same time.

Second, the identity burn. If the identity file is locally accessible on the server, it is overwritten and deleted from disk. If the device is remote and the file is not accessible — if it was lost in the field — the revocation still proceeds. The operator will be denied on their next sync attempt via the revoked flag. They cannot check in. They cannot receive updates. The network stops recognizing them.

Third, group key rotation. Every remaining active operator receives a new group encryption key on their next sync. This is the critical step. Even if the compromised device somehow retained old session material, it cannot decrypt traffic generated after the rotation. The operational picture that comes after the compromise is not visible to whoever holds the old device.

Self-Revocation

An operator who suspects their own device is compromised can initiate self-revocation from the client. This is not just an account logout. Confirmed self-revocation stops all sync immediately, closes active Reticulum links, destroys the encrypted identity files on disk, clears the enrollment metadata, and marks any pending outbound records as revoked. The device goes dark. Fresh enrollment from a clean device is required before that operator can reconnect to the network.

The audit log records the revocation event. The callsign and a hash of the RNS identity are preserved for correlation. The raw identity value does not appear in plaintext logs — even the audit record avoids unnecessary exposure.

A centralized system where all operational data lives on a shared server does not have this property. Compromise one node and you have everything it holds — past and present. TALON's distributed model means a seized device yields one device's data, protected by the passphrase, and the network can cut that identity loose and rotate its keys before the device is ever unlocked.

Why This Matters Now

The need for civilian coordination infrastructure that does not depend on commercial networks has never been more obvious. Mutual aid networks coordinate over WhatsApp groups that go dark when the towers do. Search-and-rescue teams carry radios that give them no shared operational picture. Community emergency response volunteers show up to a staging area with no way to know who else is there, what skills are present, or what has already been tried.

ATAK and ATOC are excellent tools for the organizations and scenarios they were built for. But they were not built for your neighborhood. They were not built for a rural or inner city aid group that cannot afford TAK Server infrastructure. They were not built for a mutual aid network whose members are rightly skeptical about where their data goes. They were not built from the assumption that the internet is gone.

TALON was.

The volunteer who installs a node and keeps their enrollment current is not preparing for catastrophe. They are building the habit of coordination before it is needed, so that when it is needed, it is not new. There is no skill that degrades faster under stress than an unfamiliar one. The coordination model that fails in a disaster is almost always the one that was never tested outside of one.

The Linux client is packaged and running. The server is operational. The mesh connects. The Android client is ahead. Windows packaging is ahead. The day when a community response team deploys TALON from solar-powered hardware, over long-range radio, coordinating operators across a region with no functioning internet — that day is coming.

You are not waiting for a product. You are building infrastructure for the moments when it will matter most.

And it is free.
